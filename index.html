No. IStrategyState of Fashion 2026

Luxury's recalibration year: trust, not price, is the lever.

McKinsey and BoF's tenth annual State of Fashion report — the industry's most-cited annual diagnostic — delivers a finding that should reshape how we model luxury demand. Between 2023 and 2025, roughly 80% of all luxury market growth came from price increases rather than unit-volume expansion. Translated: the sector has been running on a pricing lever, not a demand-creation lever, and that lever is running out of travel. The aspirational middle-class consumer — once the volume engine of mid-tier luxury — has been priced out, with a category of "luxury-curious" buyers effectively eradicated from the customer base. McKinsey's Gemma D'Auria explicitly frames 2026 as a year of "recalibration" driven by what she calls a "breach of trust."

The interesting wrinkle is regional. McKinsey's data show that "creativity and innovation" rank as primary purchase drivers for Chinese HNW consumers but rank surprisingly low for European HNW consumers, where heritage and craftsmanship dominate. This means a single global brand-positioning strategy now leaks utility on both ends: it under-delivers creativity to the Asian buyer and over-emphasizes novelty to the European one. The implication for brand strategy is structural — segment by cohort, not by geography. Simultaneously, a generation of local Chinese challenger brands (Laopu Gold for jewelry, Songmont for leather goods) is occupying the gap left by Western luxury's pricing out of the aspirational middle, offering credible craftsmanship and cultural specificity at price points Western houses have abandoned. For 2026, margins — not growth — are the operative variable for every executive in the sector.

A natural setting for a structural demand model with a latent "trust" state variable, or a Bayesian-persuasion framing in which price-as-quality-signal becomes informationally noisy when the price-quality link is broken repeatedly. The cross-regional heterogeneity in creativity-as-driver is itself a publishable empirical finding if you can formalize it — perhaps with a hierarchical model letting the utility weights on heritage and creativity vary by consumer cohort.
Further Reading Han, Nunes & Drèze (2010), "Signaling Status with Luxury Goods: The Role of Brand Prominence" Journal of Marketing, 74(4), 15–30. The foundational taxonomy of brand-prominence-by-consumer-type — directly relevant to how aspirational consumers being priced out reshapes signaling equilibria. journals.sagepub.com

No. IISignalingQuiet Luxury

The Row, Phoebe Philo, and the logo-free elite.

Andrea Felsted (Bloomberg, via BoF) argues that elite spenders are systematically migrating from overexposed mega-brands toward understated, logo-free luxury. The Row — the Olsen sisters' label — and Phoebe Philo's eponymous post-Celine line are the canonical exemplars: garments priced in the multi-thousands, identifiable only by silhouette and material to in-group viewers, and entirely without conspicuous branding. This is countersignaling in textbook form, formalized by Feltovich, Harbaugh & To (2002): agents at the highest level of a property invest less in proving it than those at the middle level, because their high status is already inferable from other channels (the right shipping address, the right dinner reservation, the right vocabulary). Loud branding becomes, paradoxically, a tell of insecurity.

What makes 2026 different is that the phenomenon is now commercially material at scale. The Row reportedly trades on the secondary market at retention rates closer to Hermès than to Saint Laurent. Phoebe Philo's first drops sold out within hours despite essentially zero traditional advertising. Bellezza & Berger's (2020) "Distance" framework provides additional structure here: high-status actors can safely adopt low-status signals — vintage Levi's, Toyota Camrys, no logos — precisely because their position is secure enough to withstand misinterpretation, while middle-status consumers must signal loudly to avoid being mistaken for the unestablished. The Row's appeal is therefore structurally the inverse of Gucci's: Gucci sells visibility, The Row sells the absence of it, and the same consumer cannot want both. For empirical work, succession events at major houses (Sabato De Sarno arriving at Gucci, Hedi Slimane departing Celine, etc.) function as near-ideal natural experiments on logo prominence, because they generate exogenous shifts in brand visibility without changing the underlying product category.

An event study around designer succession at major houses, measuring how logo prominence in the immediate post-succession collection affects (a) primary-market sell-through and (b) resale-market price retention, conditional on the buyer's segment. Or — structurally — a three-type signaling model (low / middle / high) where the high type prefers signal absence and the middle type prefers signal saturation, with empirical identification through observed brand-prominence variation across price tiers.
Further Reading Feltovich, Harbaugh & To (2002), "Too Cool for School? Signaling and Countersignaling" RAND Journal of Economics, 33(4), 630–649. The foundational countersignaling paper. Read it once and you will see the structure underneath every "quiet luxury" article you encounter for the next year. wiley.com

No. IIIMarketsQ1 Earnings

Kering Q1, Hermès Q1, and the Tapestry–Kering inversion.

The Q1 numbers are unkind to luxury's incumbents and revelatory about its valuation logic. Kering reported Q1 revenue of €3.57B, down 6.2% year-over-year, with Gucci — still the group's flagship — coming in at €1.35B, down 14.3%. That is now seven consecutive quarters of Gucci decline despite a new creative director and a high-profile relaunch. Hermès posted €4.1B in Q1, up 5.6% YoY, but the surprise was that this represented an analyst miss: Hermès has trained the market to expect double-digit growth, and the company cited Middle East disruption as the proximate cause. BNP Paribas now forecasts ~6% organic growth for the sector in 2026, with the United States as the only major region carrying both short- and medium-term positive catalysts.

The deeper signal is the Tapestry–Kering inversion: Tapestry (parent of Coach, Kate Spade, Stuart Weitzman) now trades at a market capitalization nearly matching Kering's. Five years ago this comparison would have been unthinkable — Kering's Gucci-Bottega-Saint-Laurent-Balenciaga portfolio commanded a multiple roughly 3x Tapestry's. The convergence is being driven by two simultaneous forces: Coach's successful repositioning as a craft-led "expressive luxury" brand resonating strongly with Gen Z (who now account for a disproportionate share of its growth), and Gucci's prolonged identity crisis. Translated: the market is pricing "accessible-premium with strong Gen Z affinity" comparably to traditional ultra-luxury. If that holds, several priors about luxury's pricing power need updating. The most important is the assumption that brand equity, once built at the top tier, is durably defensible against challenges from below — Coach's trajectory suggests it isn't.

Build a hedonic regression of market cap on a vector of brand attributes (heritage age, logo prominence, Gen-Z share of buyers, regional revenue mix, creative-director tenure). The residual is a measure of "narrative premium" — the part of valuation not explained by observable brand fundamentals. The interesting object is then what predicts the residual: investor sentiment? Earnings-call language? Search trends?
Further Reading Simon & Sullivan (1993), "The Measurement and Determinants of Brand Equity: A Financial Approach" Marketing Science, 12(1), 28–52. The canonical paper on extracting brand equity from financial market data — methodologically right next to what you'd need for the Kering / Tapestry question. informs.org

No. IVMethodsNBER Working Paper

Ads as cues: a memory-based theory of brand habit.

Bordalo, Burro, and co-authors (NBER WP 34387) develop a model that reframes one of the longest-standing debates in marketing economics: what does advertising actually do? The dominant tradition — going back to Marshall and formalized by Becker-Murphy — treats advertising as either an informational channel (it tells consumers a product exists or has certain attributes) or a persuasive channel (it directly enters the utility function, shifting the marginal utility of consumption). Bordalo et al. propose a third mechanism rooted in cognitive psychology: advertising works as a memory cue, retrieving prior consumption experiences from memory rather than altering preferences directly. The mathematical apparatus draws on the "Diagnostic Expectations" tradition Bordalo developed with Gennaioli and Shleifer for finance, now applied to consumer choice.

The model yields two empirical predictions that are surprisingly sharp and testable. First, similarity matters non-trivially: a Pepsi ad reminds Pepsi drinkers of Pepsi, but it also reminds Coke drinkers of Coke, because the cue (carbonation, summer, refreshment) retrieves whichever memory is strongest for that consumer. So ad similarity reinforces habits and reduces cross-brand mobility precisely when products are most economically substitutable — the opposite of what a standard utility model would predict. Second, ads that are dissimilar to a brand's prior advertising create negative own-brand spillovers via memory interference, because they crowd out retrieval of the brand's consolidated memory representation. Together with Bronnenberg & Dubé's (2017) framework on persistent brand capital — which documents that brand preferences exhibit decades-long persistence inconsistent with naive learning models — these two papers form a candidate citation backbone for any branding paper you write in the next two years. The Bordalo paper specifically suggests that the right unit of analysis is not the ad-impression but the ad-impression × consumer-memory-state.

The prediction that ad similarity reinforces habits while reducing cross-brand mobility maps cleanly onto luxury, where category-level visual conventions are exceptionally strong — the campaign aesthetics of Saint Laurent, Celine, and Bottega Veneta have converged to a near-indistinguishable minimalism. A test using CLIP or comparable image embeddings to measure ad-similarity across luxury campaigns, then correlating with brand-switching data, could be original and tractable. Bonus: the framework predicts that brand-distinctive advertising (Gucci's maximalism under Alessandro Michele) should be more effective per-impression than category-conventional advertising — a falsifiable claim.
Further Reading Bronnenberg & Dubé (2017), "The Formation of Consumer Brand Preferences" Annual Review of Economics, vol. 9, 353–382. The companion theoretical apparatus to Bordalo et al. — read together they cover the modern empirical literature on brand capital, including the puzzling persistence of preferences across generations and migrations. nber.org/papers/w22691

No. VMethodsCausal ML

Causal ML for marketing ROI: two recent frameworks.

The CATE (Conditional Average Treatment Effect) literature has moved from theoretical novelty in 2015 (Wager-Athey) to standard toolkit in 2026, and two early-2026 conference papers crystallize where applied marketing has landed. Marmonier & Aranda (IDEAL 2025, published January 2026) combine matching, inverse-probability weighting, and causal forests to estimate heterogeneous Average Treatment Effects on the Treated (ATTs) across campaign-feature subgroups — useful because it sidesteps the standard assumption that treatment effects are constant across consumers, which is empirically almost always wrong. Ho & Lam (ICCIES 2026) propose a multi-stage Robust Causal Inference Framework that uses propensity-score estimation followed by an ensemble of causal meta-learners (S-Learner, T-Learner, X-Learner) to estimate CATE in a direct-marketing context, with Random-Forest-based propensity scoring achieving AUC of 0.62 — modest but enough to satisfy the positivity assumption (overlap range 0.05–0.46).

What is methodologically noteworthy is that both papers are rigorous on the pre-treatment assumption, which is where most applied marketing work cuts corners. Ho-Lam explicitly excludes "call duration" as a leakage feature — a small detail that signals real care, because call duration is post-treatment and including it would induce massive bias inflating apparent treatment effects. This is exactly the kind of mistake referees in QME and Marketing Science now flag aggressively. The substantive limitation of both papers is that the heterogeneity discovered is descriptive rather than explanatory: they show that different subgroups respond differently, but offer no theory for why. If you want to publish quickly, the Ho-Lam framework is a defensible empirical template you could lift wholesale. If you want to publish well, the gap is wide open for an application that pairs meta-learner CATE estimation with a clean economic theory of heterogeneity — for instance, grounding the heterogeneity in past brand-relationship state, in signaling type (à la Han-Nunes-Drèze), or in habit strength (à la Bordalo et al.). That theoretical anchoring is what separates a JMR paper from a workshop paper.

The literature is hungry for an application that combines meta-learner CATE with a theory of heterogeneity grounded in something other than demographics or RFM. Candidates: past brand-relationship state, signaling type, habit strength à la Bordalo, or — for luxury — buyer's position in the status hierarchy. The payoff to theoretical anchoring is high right now because the field has too much applied work and too little theory.
Further Reading Wager & Athey (2018), "Estimation and Inference of Heterogeneous Treatment Effects Using Random Forests" Journal of the American Statistical Association, 113(523), 1228–1242. The foundational causal-forest paper. Every applied CATE paper you'll read cites this; understanding the inference machinery (honest splitting, asymptotic normality) is non-optional for credible empirical work. arxiv.org/abs/1510.04342

No. VIFrontierAI & Discovery

AI shopping agents as a structural disruption.

McKinsey's Anita Balchandani makes a claim with deep implications for marketing economics: consumers are increasingly routing product discovery through AI assistants (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity), and this shifts the unit of competition from "search-result ranking" to "position in an LLM's generated recommendation". The successor concept to SEO is being called Generative Engine Optimization (GEO), and it asks a fundamentally different question. SEO is about being on the first page of ten blue links; GEO is about being mentioned, favorably, in a single synthesized paragraph. Bain reports that approximately 60% of searches in 2025 ended without the user clicking through to any destination — a number that should reorient how we model the consumer purchase funnel.

The disruption is asymmetric. Direct-to-consumer brands with strong organic content footprints may benefit; their owned media and review ecosystems feed LLM training corpora. Multi-brand aggregators and platforms face a more existential threat, because LLMs can synthesize across the same product universe without the platform's UX, eliminating the platform's mediating function. For luxury specifically, the question is whether LLMs will preserve or flatten the prestige hierarchy. If a model trained on web text is asked "what's a good handbag for a wedding," it might recommend Hermès and Mansur Gavriel in the same breath, treating them as substitutes when they are objects of fundamentally different signaling games. This is not a hypothetical concern — Item IX documents that LLMs already systematically miscategorize brand tier. The research frontier here is unusually green: there is essentially no canonical theory of brand competition under LLM-mediated discovery, which means a well-executed first-mover paper could define the field's vocabulary for the next decade. The companion methodological frontier — using LLMs as research instruments rather than research subjects — is being staked out by Brand, Israeli & Ngwe (2023) at HBS, who demonstrate that LLMs can extract consumer preferences from text in ways that approximate human survey responses, with the critical caveat that the response distribution matters as much as the mean.

A first-mover paper could define the field's vocabulary. Audit how a frontier LLM recommends luxury items across structured prompts — varying occasion, price range, signaling intent — and measure: are heritage brands over-recommended relative to market share? Is there a recency bias toward recently-trending brands? Is there a price-tier ceiling at which LLMs stop differentiating brands? The descriptive paper alone is publishable. The strategic paper modeling firm investment in "LLM visibility" as a new form of marketing capital is the bigger contribution.
Further Reading Brand, Israeli & Ngwe (2023), "Using LLMs for Market Research" Harvard Business School Working Paper 23-062. Establishes how GPT-class models can be used to extract consumer preferences, and explicitly addresses the distributional structure of LLM responses — methodologically essential for any GEO or AI-discovery paper, because the variance in LLM outputs across prompt phrasings is itself an object of study. hbs.edu

No. VIISignalingAesthetic Polarity

Maximalism's commercial resurgence vs. quiet luxury.

QArt Solutions' B2B order-pattern data — gathered across luxury distribution channels — shows that statement pieces, bold tailoring, and architectural outerwear have moved from niche commercial categories to core priority for 2026 buys. The shift is empirically substantial, not anecdotal: the data shows the strongest sell-through coming from categories that "deliver an immediate, legible identity signal to the end consumer." Alessandro Michele's appointment at Valentino, Jean Paul Gaultier's continued theatricality, and Comme des Garçons' sculptural excess all confirm the trajectory. The proximate driver is generational: Gen Z controls an estimated $400 billion in identity-signaling spend globally, and digital platforms structurally reward high-impact, visually disruptive content over restrained editorial styling.

Now set this against Item II (logo-free quiet luxury) and you have what looks like a paradox but is actually a perfectly clean Bourdieu-Veblen segmentation: maximalism for the aspirational consumer who needs to signal arrival; minimalism for the established consumer who needs to signal arrival-was-never-in-doubt. Same calendar year, opposite signaling strategies, segmented by consumer type and security of status position. This is precisely the structure that a formal two-type signaling model is built to capture, and the empirical landscape — with simultaneous commercial success for both Gucci's loud aesthetic and The Row's silent one — is unusually favorable for identification. The Bellezza, Gino & Keinan (2014) "Red Sneakers" framework provides additional micro-foundation: deliberate nonconformity reads as competence-and-status only when the observer can rule out the possibility that the nonconformity is unintentional. The Row gets away with logo-free minimalism because the price tag forecloses the unintentional reading; a teenager in plain clothes does not. That conditional recognition is the entire signaling apparatus.

A formal signaling model with two consumer types and observable-versus-unobservable luxury cues could publish well in QME or Marketing Science, especially if paired with an empirical test using image embeddings of recent runway shows to quantify aesthetic dispersion across price tiers and consumer cohorts. The deeper claim — that aesthetic polarization in a single category is diagnostic of status-segmentation in the consumer base — is testable across categories (sneakers, watches, wine) and would generalize beyond fashion.
Further Reading Bellezza, Gino & Keinan (2014), "The Red Sneakers Effect: Inferring Status and Competence from Signals of Nonconformity" Journal of Consumer Research, 41(1), 35–54. A modern empirical anchor for the idea that breaking aesthetic conventions can itself be a status move — conditional on observers being able to rule out incompetence. Foundational for any quiet-vs-loud paper. academic.oup.com

No. VIIIStructureCircular Economy

Resale, repair, and circularity as a margin lever.

Deloitte's 2026 Global Powers of Luxury report contains numbers that should reshape how we think about luxury's industrial structure: 68.3% of luxury fashion companies now offer repair or refurbishment services, 53.8% run certified pre-owned or trade-in programs, and 44.5% partner directly with third-party resale platforms. Five years ago these numbers were below 20%. Euromonitor reframes the shift as moving from "sustainability-as-compliance" to "sustainability-as-ROI" — a meaningful repositioning, because it implies brands now view circular-economy investment as a margin-positive activity rather than a cost of regulatory and reputational hygiene. Deloitte further reports that 66.9% of luxury executives surveyed expect stable or growing revenues in 2026, and 70.7% expect to maintain or improve margins; the latter number requires a margin lever, and circularity is increasingly that lever.

For Veblen goods, this is a non-trivial structural change with effects that propagate through both primary and secondary markets. A brand-controlled resale channel commits the brand to a credible secondary-market price, which feeds back into primary-market willingness-to-pay: if a consumer knows a Birkin will retain 90% of its primary-market price on the official Hermès pre-owned program, the effective cost of ownership drops and willingness-to-pay rises. Importantly, this only works for goods whose value is anchored in scarcity and signaling rather than functional utility — the resale-price-as-floor logic doesn't apply to a Toyota Camry. The Birkin's near-perfect price retention across the primary-secondary boundary has historically been an emergent property of the secondary market; what happens when that property gets actively managed by the issuing brand is genuinely understudied. The economic question is whether brand-controlled resale is complementary to primary-market pricing (it increases certainty about future value) or substitutive (it erodes scarcity by making used inventory more accessible). The answer almost certainly depends on the elasticity of new-product demand to expected resale value, which is empirically estimable from product-launch events with and without simultaneous pre-owned program launches.

A two-period model with endogenous resale supply, where the brand chooses primary-market quantity and resale-channel participation jointly. The tension to formalize: more resale availability increases primary-market access (good for revenue), but erodes scarcity (bad for signaling and therefore for willingness-to-pay). The equilibrium balance is publishable, especially if you can identify it empirically off the staggered rollout of brand-controlled pre-owned programs across the Deloitte panel.
Further Reading Amaldoss & Jain (2005), "Conspicuous Consumption and Sophisticated Thinking" Management Science, 51(10), 1449–1466. A formal model of Veblen-good pricing under heterogeneous consumer sophistication — the natural baseline before introducing a brand-controlled resale channel. informs.org

No. IXFrontierHBR · Agentic AI

When LLMs miscategorize your brand.

Harvard Business Review (March 2026) reports a finding that should be unsettling to anyone in luxury strategy: two-thirds of Gen Z consumers and over half of Millennials now use LLMs to research products before purchase. Gokcen Karaca, head of digital at Pernod Ricard, commissioned a formal audit of how leading models (GPT, Claude, Gemini) represented his brands. The findings: systematic mis-categorization at the brand-tier level. Ballantine's — an affordable mass-market Scotch that retails for under $30 — was repeatedly classified by major LLMs as a prestige product, conflated with single-malts that retail for $100+. The error pattern was not random noise; it appeared consistently across prompts and across multiple LLM vendors.

This is the operationalization of Item VI's theoretical concern. The empirical fact is that LLMs are now the medium of brand discovery for younger consumers, and they get the brand-tier mapping wrong in non-random, predictable ways. For luxury specifically, this matters acutely because prestige is a positional good: its value derives from being ranked above other goods, not from any absolute property. An LLM that flattens or scrambles the prestige hierarchy reshapes the consumer's choice set in ways that propagate into purchase. If Claude tells a Gen Z consumer that Ballantine's is prestige, the consumer's reference point for what "prestige" means shifts, and so does their willingness-to-pay for Macallan. Worse: LLM training corpora are persistent and update slowly, so corrections to these errors lag by months or years. The strategic response from brands is non-obvious. Traditional advertising shapes consumer beliefs directly; shaping LLM training data is a fundamentally different game, requiring brands to think about long-form web content, editorial coverage, and third-party reviews not as marketing channels but as inputs to a future model. This is "LLM visibility" or "training-data presence" as a marketing capital category that did not exist five years ago.

An audit study: for a representative basket of luxury and premium brands, ask a frontier LLM to (a) rank prestige tier, (b) recommend for various occasions, (c) estimate retail price, (d) suggest substitutes. Compare to actual market prices and to expert-rated prestige rankings. The distributional pattern of errors — over-prestige bias for heritage names, under-prestige bias for newer entrants, perhaps cultural-encoding bias as in Item X — is the paper, and the methodology travels to any consumer category.
Further Reading Li, Zhang, Wang & Gao (2024), "Frontiers: Determining the Validity of Large Language Models for Automated Perceptual Analysis" Marketing Science, 43(4), 665–679. Tests whether LLMs can substitute for survey-based brand perception studies — directly relevant to any audit of LLM-mediated brand discovery, because it establishes the validity threshold below which LLM outputs cannot be trusted as proxies for human perception. informs.org

No. XMethodsarXiv · LLM Bias

Cultural encoding and the Existence Gap in LLM brand discovery.

A recent arXiv paper introduces a concept that names something the field has been circling without articulating: the "Existence Gap" — a brand's absence from AI-generated recommendations due to insufficient presence in the model's training data, regardless of the brand's actual market quality or consumer relevance. The empirical centerpiece is a controlled experiment using 1,909 pure-English query-LLM pairs across categories. The result: Chinese LLMs (Qwen3, DeepSeek, Doubao) recommend brands at an 88.9% rate, vs. 58.3% for international LLMs (GPT, Claude, Gemini), on identical English-language queries. The 30.6 percentage-point gap is statistically robust at χ² = 226.60, p < .001. For specific Chinese brands, the asymmetry is even starker: Zhizibianjie sees a 0% mention rate across 32 queries on international LLMs vs. 65.6% on Chinese LLMs (φ = 0.58, very large effect size).

This is structurally a much harder problem than traditional search engine bias. With search, a brand can theoretically buy its way to visibility through SEM or build organic ranking through content. With LLM training corpora, the brand is either present in the data moat or it isn't — and the asymmetry is locked in by training cycles measured in months or years, not adjustable in real time. The authors propose calling the relevant marketing capital "Data Moat depth": the quantity and quality of AI-visible content about a brand in the training corpora of frontier models. This reframes brand-building as a long-game investment in editorial coverage, third-party reviews, and discoverable web presence — not because these channels reach consumers directly (most don't, in volume), but because they reach the model, which then reaches the consumer at scale. For luxury, where Western houses dominate Western corpora, the existing asymmetry works in incumbents' favor; for emerging-market challenger brands, it is a structural market-entry barrier invisible to traditional Porter-style competitive analysis. The paper's deeper conceptual contribution is that this barrier operates independently of language: a Chinese brand with strong English-language web presence is visible to international LLMs; a Chinese brand with strong Chinese-only web presence is not, even when the user is querying in English. Cultural encoding is therefore a function of training data, not of consumer linguistics.

Two clean angles: (1) a descriptive paper quantifying the Existence Gap across product categories and LLM vendors, ideally with a panel structure across model versions to capture how the gap evolves with training updates; (2) a strategic paper modeling firm investment in "training-data presence" as a new form of marketing capital, distinct from advertising but with similar persistence properties — perhaps formalizable as a goodwill-stock model à la Nerlove-Arrow with depreciation tied to model-retraining cadence.
Further Reading "Cultural Encoding in Large Language Models: The Existence Gap in AI-Mediated Brand Discovery" (2026) arXiv preprint 2601.00869. The source paper. Worth reading the methodology section closely — the audit-study design (controlled query variation, multi-model comparison, χ² inference) is highly reusable for adjacent research questions in luxury, beauty, and consumer electronics. arxiv.org/pdf/2601.00869
